Voltage Measurement Correlation Report

Aircraft N238PS (Diamond DA40NG) — Garmin G1000 NXi vs Triplett VDL48
Date of flights: February 8, 2026  |  Report generated: February 09, 2026

Key Finding: The G1000 NXi consistently under-reports bus voltage compared to an independent reference logger. The mean offset is -1.36 V (G1000 reads lower), with the difference being highly statistically significant (paired t-test, p < 0.001). This under-reading is sufficient to trigger spurious LOW VOLTS annunciations during normal alternator operation.

1. Test Setup

A Triplett VDL48 voltage data logger (2-second sampling) was connected to the aircraft electrical bus as an independent reference. Two flights were conducted with the G1000 NXi data logging enabled (1-second sampling):

2. VDL48 Full Recording Overview

The VDL48 shows stable ~28.3 V during both flights (alternator charging), a gradual decay to ~25.5 V during the engine-off idle period, and 0 V after disconnection.

3. Flight-by-Flight Voltage Comparison

The green trace (VDL48 reference) remains steady at ~28.3 V during both flights. The blue trace (G1000 volt1) consistently reads lower and exhibits substantially more fluctuation. The red trace shows the instantaneous difference.

Observation: The G1000 voltage trace shows frequent transient dips not present in the VDL reference. These dips are consistent with variable voltage drop across a resistive connection in the G1000's measurement or ground path. The worst-case dip reaches -5.6 V below the VDL reading.

5. Distribution of Differences

Both flights show distributions shifted well below zero, confirming the G1000 systematically reads lower. Flight 1 shows a larger mean offset (-1.869 V) than Flight 2 (-0.990 V), suggesting the magnitude varies with operating conditions.

6. Correlation Scatter Plot

Nearly all data points fall below the 1:1 line, confirming the systematic under-reading. The low r-squared value indicates the G1000 fluctuations are largely independent of actual bus voltage changes, pointing to an issue in the G1000's voltage sensing path rather than a simple calibration offset.

7. Interpretation and Probable Cause

The data pattern is consistent with a high-resistance connection in the G1000's voltage measurement or ground return path. Key evidence:

  • Variable offset, not constant: A calibration error would produce a fixed offset. The observed difference varies from -5.6 V to +1.7 V, with a standard deviation of 0.71 V.
  • G1000 shows excess noise: The VDL sees a stable bus (0.268 V std dev) while the G1000 fluctuates much more (0.686 V std dev). The extra variance comes from current-dependent voltage drops across a resistive connection.
  • Near-zero correlation (r = 0.09): The two instruments are measuring the same bus, yet their readings are essentially uncorrelated. This means the G1000's voltage fluctuations are driven by its own ground/sensing path impedance, not by actual bus voltage changes.
  • Transient deep dips: Momentary dips to -5.6 V below reference are consistent with high-current events (radio transmit, servo actuation) pulling current through a resistive ground, causing instantaneous offset spikes.
  • Different magnitude between flights: Flight 1 mean offset was -1.87 V vs Flight 2 at -0.99 V. Thermal expansion, vibration settling, or connector seating changes between flights can alter contact resistance.

8. Recommended Actions

  • Inspect G1000 GDU and GIA unit ground terminals at the airframe ground bus for corrosion, loose hardware, paint under ring terminals, or cracked terminals.
  • Measure resistance from the G1000 ground pin (at the connector) to battery negative. Values above ~0.02-0.05 ohms would explain the observed offset (at 20 A load, 0.05 ohms = 1.0 V drop).
  • Inspect the main airframe ground bus to battery/engine ground strap.
  • Check the G1000 harness connector pins (GDU 1050/1060, GIA 63W) for the voltage sensing and ground pins specifically.
  • After repair, repeat this test to verify the offset is eliminated.
